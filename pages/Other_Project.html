Enhancing Wind Energy Forecasting for Cost Efficiency in Hybrid Diesel and Solar Power Plants Using Artificial Neural Networks and Meteoblue Weather Data

This study integrates Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) with Meteoblue Weather data to improve the performance of hybrid diesel and solar power plants by accurately predicting wind energy output. Focusing on the Derawan Islands in Indonesia, where traditional energy grids are challenging to establish, the research enhances energy management by adding wind power to reduce the load on diesel plants. The ANN model, trained on historical weather data, achieved high predictive accuracy with an R² value of 0.9969 and RMSE values of 0.5772 for training and 0.5620 for testing. These results indicate a strong correlation between predicted and actual wind speeds, outperforming similar models used in other regions like Ethiopia and Chile. The findings highlight the ANN model's effectiveness in optimizing wind energy integration, making hybrid power systems more reliable and cost-effective for remote areas.

The correlation heatmap highlights several key variables with a strong correlation (greater than 0.5) with wind speed at 100 meters. Notably, wind speeds at 10 and 100 meters exhibit a perfect positive correlation with each other (correlation coefficient of 1), indicating a consistent relationship across these altitudes. Wind gusts also show a strong positive correlation (0.87) with wind speed at 100 meters, suggesting that higher gust speeds are closely associated with higher average wind speeds at this height. Evapotranspiration has a significant positive correlation (0.75) with wind speed at 100 meters, indicating that increased wind speeds may enhance the evapotranspiration rate. Additionally, the Planetary Boundary Layer (PBL) height shows a moderate positive correlation (0.57) with wind speed at 100 meters, suggesting that higher PBL heights are associated with higher wind speeds. These strong correlations underscore the key variables influencing wind speed at 100 meters, providing valuable insights for wind energy forecasting and related applications.

Anomaly Detection in Kwh meter using Autoencoder

This project implements an Anomaly Detection system for Kwh meters using a deep learning Autoencoder architecture. The core objective is to identify abnormal electricity usage patterns, such as meter tampering or technical malfunctions, which often result in energy loss. By training the model exclusively on "Normal" consumption data, the Autoencoder learns an efficient compressed representation of typical usage.

The implementation begins with the deep learning phase, Specifically, the Training and Validation Loss Curve visualizes the reduction of the Mean Squared Error (MSE) during the learning process over 50 epochs. The results show a steady and consistent convergence, where the validation loss remains in close proximity to the training loss. This stability is critical as it indicates that the model has effectively generalized the consumption features rather than over-fitting to noise, providing a robust mathematical foundation for identifying future deviations in energy consumption behavior.

Training and Validation Loss Curve

Normal Test Data vs Decoder Output (Low Reconstruction Error)

To validate the model's reconstructive capabilities, it is first tested against a fresh batch of normal consumption data that it has not encountered during training. As shown in the comparison graph, the Decoder Output (Red) closely follows the actual Normal Test Data (Blue). The resulting "Gap" or reconstruction error is negligible, signifying that the model correctly perceives this data as part of the normal distribution. By maintaining a low and predictable reconstruction error during these windows, the system ensures high operational transparency. This baseline performance is the cornerstone of the detection algorithm, as any significant increase in this localized error will serve as the primary trigger for anomaly classification.

The core strength of the Autoencoder approach is revealed when the system is challenged with abnormal usage patterns, such as those caused by meter bypassing or unauthorized technical malfunctions. Because the model was never exposed to these erratic patterns during its training phase, it fails to find an efficient compressed representation for them. Consequently, the Decoder attempts to reconstruct the input based on its "normal" logic, resulting in a dramatic and sustained discrepancy between the Input (Blue) and the Output (Red). This high reconstruction error is a clear indicator of fraudulent activity or hardware failure, allowing utility providers to move from random inspections to targeted, evidence-based forensic investigations.

Extended Comparison of Abnormal Test Data and Decoder Output

Analyzing the system's performance over an extended time horizon is essential for distinguishing between isolated measurement noise and persistent anomalies. The extended comparison graph demonstrates how the model maintains a high reconstruction gap throughout the entire duration of an anomalous event. This longitudinal stability proves that the detection signal is not affected by short-term fluctuations in the grid or minor sensor errors. By observing the cumulative error over time, the algorithm can apply a confidence threshold that only flags events with sustained high reconstruction gaps, significantly reducing the burden on data analysts by prioritizing high-impact energy loss events.

The overall efficacy of the machine learning model is empirically summarized in the Confusion Matrix. This matrix details the distribution of True Positives, True Negatives, False Positives, and False Negatives obtained after applying the classification threshold. With a high number of True Positives (correctly identified anomalies) and a very low rate of False Positives, the model demonstrates high precision and recall. This empirical validation ensures that utility providers can trust the system's alerts for real-world grid monitoring, effectively catching theft while avoiding expensive and unnecessary field inspections caused by false alarms.
